Introduction

Sympathetic postganglionic neurons (SPNs), the last common motor output of the sympathetic nervous system, pass converged motor commands from the spinal cord to downstream muscles and visceral organs. SPNs located in the thoracic region (tSPNs) of the paravertebral sympathetic chain ganglia control vasomotor function to regulate vasculature in upper and middle extremities [1-2], and dysfunction of tSPNs is implicated in various autonomic disorders such as autonomic dysreflexia [3]. tSPNs are difficult to access in vivo study due to the adherent fat surrounding them, so their cellular properties are often inferred from SPNs located in the cervical and lumbar regions [4-8]. There are only 3 in vitro studies of the electrophysiological properties of tSPN in the vertebrate system [9-11], and they all employed a sharp microelectrode recording approach. However, recent studies show that sharp microelectrode recordings introduce electrode impalement-induced leak conductance, rendering less accurate recordings [6,11].
